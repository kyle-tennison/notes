# R7. Fasteners


## Terminology 

![](https://www.afi.cc/customer/asfain/Hubspot%20Files/Hubspot%20Site/Files%20Uploaded%20from%20Hubspot/Screw%20thread-1.jpg)

The *pitch* of a bolt is the distance between adjacent threads, which is also the *reciprocal* of the number of threads per unit length.

A *bolt* is designed to be used with a nut, a *screw* is designed to be threaded into a part. In reality, screws are sometimes used with nuts—but the screw is still a screw.

The *tensile stress area* refers to the area of an equivalent unthreaded rod that would yield the same tensile strength. These are generally experimentally determined and can be looked up for a given nominal diameter.

The unified thread standard names bolts using numbers when the nominal diameter is under 1/4"; afterwards, it uses the nominal diameter. 

The following is an example spec:

$${\small \frac{5}{18}}\text{-}18 \text{ UNF}\times 2 \text{ HEX HD CAP SCR}$$

where:
- $5/18$ is the major diameter. If it were under $1/4$, it would be a number.
- $18$ is the number of threads per inch
- $\rm UNF$ refers to the Unified Fine Series. This could instead be $\rm UNC$ for the coarse series.
- $2$ is the length
- $\text{HEX HD CAP SCR}$ refers to the type of fastener

The *grip* of the bolt refers to everything that that sits in the fastened portion of the bolt, including washers. This will always be a little smaller than the length of the bolt [^1].

## Preloading

### Spring Constant

A spring constant $k$ gives the (assumed to be) constant relationship between displacement and force. For a material with a constant cross section of area $A$, length $l$, and elasticity $E$, the deformation/displacement $\delta$ between ends is:

$$\delta = \frac{Fl}{AE}$$

Thus, the spring constant is:

$$k = \frac{F}{\delta} = \frac{AE}{l} $$

### Member Stiffness

The stiffness of the bolt itself $k_b$ is easy to find, given it is a single part:

$$k_b = \frac{\pi d^2 E}{4l}$$

where $d$ is the *major* diameter.

> Not sure why the major diameter is used here instead of $A_t$, but this is what Shigley recommends. 

There is often more than one member in the grip of the bolt; when this is the case, the total spring constant of the members $k_m$ is found by summing the individual members like springs in parallel:

$$\frac{1}{k_m} =  \frac{1}{k_1}+\frac{1}{k_2}+\frac{1}{k_3}+\cdots$$

When there is a soft gasket, the stiffness is so low that it ends up dominating the above equation, so the stiffness of the other members is okay to neglect for all practical purposes. 

It can be hard to find $k_1,k_2,\dots$, so one assumption is:

$$k_m = \frac{2\pi d^2E}{l}$$

which assumes that the gripped members are a bored cylinder with an $\rm ID$ of $d$ and an $\rm OD$ of $3d$. Apparently this is a decent approximation.

The bolt is under load $P$, which gets partially distributed to the bolt as $P_b$ and partially distributed to the members as $P_m$, such that:

$$P = P_b + P_m$$

The deflection in the bolt $\delta_b$ is a function of the previously mentioned bolt spring constant $k_b$ and the portion of the load distributed to the bolt $P_b$:

$$\Delta \delta _b = \frac{P_b}{k_b}$$

The bolt's preload already puts it in tension, and additional $P_b$ further increases this tension.

For the members, the delta looks similar:

$$\Delta \delta_m = \frac{P_m}{k_m}$$

However, the *preload puts the members in compression*, the applied load *decreases* their existing deformation by putting them in tension.

If the members do not separate from each other, then it is true that the change in bolt deflection $\Delta \delta_b$ is the same as the change in member deflection $\Delta \delta_m$. Therefore, it can be said that:

$$\frac{P_b}{k_b}= \frac{P_m}{k_m}, \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad P_b = \frac{k_bP_m}{k_m}= \frac{k_b(P-P_b)}{k_m}=\frac{k_bP}{k_b+k_m}$$

Sometimes, the term is pulled out such that:
$$P_b = \frac{k_b}{k_m+k_b}P = CP, \qquad C = \frac{k_b}{k_m+k_b}$$

With this definition:
$$P_m = (1-C)P$$

where $C$ is the *joint constant*.


> Not sure how that last equality is derived, but the book claims it.

The total force acting on the bolt is:

$$F_b=P_b+F_i = F_i+CP$$

where $F_i$ is the preload, in absence of any applied $P$.

The total force on the members is:

$$F_m = \frac{k_mP}{k_b+k_m}-F_i = F_i-(1-C)P$$

Hence, a higher pre-load causes a lower force exertion in the members.

This is all only true under the assumption that the members stay in contact during loading; the load required to separate the members can be found by setting $F_m=0$; at this point, the net force changes from compression to tension. Solving this gives:

$$P_0 = \frac{F_i}{1-C}$$

This can be used to develop a factor of safety:

$$N_{\rm sep} = \frac{P_0}{P}=\frac{F_i}{P(1-C)}$$
