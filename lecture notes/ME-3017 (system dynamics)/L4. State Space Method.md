# L4. State Space Method

Sometimes referred to as "Modern control."

## Pros and Cons

Pros:

- Works directly in the time domain
- Can support multiple inputs and outputs
- Can handle nonlinear systems

Cons:

- lowkey kinda hard

## Equations

The *State Equation* is:

$$\dot{ \bf{x}} = {\bf A}{\bf x} + \bf{B}\bf{u}$$

where
- $\bf A$ is the ($n \times n$) state matrix
- $\bf B$ is the ($n \times m$) input matrix
- $\bf x$ is the ($n \times 1$) state vector 
- $\bf u$ is the ($m \times 1$) input vector

The *Output Equation* is:

$${\bf y} = {\bf C x} + {\bf D u}$$

where 
- $\bf C$ is the ($p \times n$) output matrix
- $\bf D$ is the ($p \times m$) direct transmission matrix
- $\bf x$ and $\bf u$ are the same as in the state equation

## Solving systems

After making a free-body diagram and solving for $\ddot x$, you will have a system of equations in terms of: $x_i$ and $\dot x_i$. For all of the $x_i$ you are interested in:

$${\bf x} = \begin{bmatrix}
x_1\\\dot x_1\\x_2\\\dot x_2 \\\vdots
\end{bmatrix}, \qquad
\dot{\bf x} = \begin{bmatrix}
\dot x_1\\\ddot x_1\\ \dot x_2\\\ddot x_2 \\\vdots
\end{bmatrix}
$$

It's trivial to see that $\dot{\bf x}_1 = {\bf x}_2$ (in this case, might be slightly different in other case). The other relationships for the second order derivatives can be expanded from the newton's law solution. Then, you just express the relationship with:

$$\dot{ \bf{x}} = {\bf A}{\bf x} + \bf{B}\bf{u}$$

### Example

A cart with a spring and damper oscillating in the $y$ direction will have the following solution to newton's second law:

$$m\ddot y = -b\dot y - ky + u$$

In this instance, we are interested in finding $y$ and $\dot y$, because this will allow $\dot {\bf x}$ to be in terms of $\ddot y$ which is known. In the case of a massless object, we would only be interested in $y$ because $\ddot y$ could not be known; instead we would only use $\dot y$. 

But, for this simple case, we have:

$${\bf x} = \begin{bmatrix}\,y \,\\ \dot y \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \dot{\bf x} = \begin{bmatrix}\,\dot y \,\\ \ddot y \end{bmatrix}$$

Solving for $\ddot y$ gives:

$$\ddot y = - \frac km y - \frac bm \dot y +\frac 1m u$$

So we can say:

$$\dot {\bf x}_1 = {\bf x}_2 $$

and 

$$\dot{\bf x}_2  = - \frac km {\bf x}_1 - \frac bm {\bf x}_2 + \frac 1m u$$

So, putting this in matrix form is:

$$\dot{\bf x} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}0 & 1\\ -k/m & -b/m \end{bmatrix}}_{\large \bf A}
{\bf x} + \underbrace{\left[ \matrix{0\\ 1/m} \right]}_{\large \bf B} u
$$

To get $\bf C$ and $\bf D$ it's as simple as:

$$y = [1 \quad 0] \underbrace{\left[\matrix{\,y\, \\ \dot y}\right]}_{\large \bf C}+ \underbrace{0}_{\large \bf D}\cdot u$$


## State Space Solution

To solve for $x(t)$ form the state space, you use:

$$x(t) = e^{\mathbf A t} x(0) + \int \limits_0^t e^{\mathbf A (t-\tau)}\mathbf B \mathbf u(\tau)\,d\tau$$

and for $y(t)$ it is simply:

$$y(t) = \mathbf C x(t) + \mathbf D u(t)$$



## Eigenvalues

If you assert:

$$\left|\lambda \mathbf I - \mathbf A\right|=0$$

then expand, you'll get a *characteristic polynomial* in terms of $\lambda$. Solving for $\lambda$ gives the eigenvalues $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$; these values are actually equal to the poles of the transfer function $G(s)$, which is:

$$G(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)}= \mathbf C (s\mathbf I  - \mathbf A)^{-1}\mathbf B + \mathbf D$$

where $Y(s)$ and $U(s)$ are the Laplace transforms of $y(t)$ and $u(t)$. 

## Transfer Function to S.S. 

If given a transfer function $G(s)$, you can:

$$G(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{Y(s)}{X(s)}\cdot \frac{X(s)}{U(s)}$$

then take the inverse laplace.

## Stability

If you can find a matrix $\mathbf P$ such taht:

$$\hat {\bf A} = \mathbf P^{-1} \mathbf A \mathbf P =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_1& & & 0\\
& \lambda_2 & & & \\
& & \ddots & \\
0 & & & \lambda_3
\end{bmatrix}
$$

And:

$$x = 
\begin{bmatrix}
z_1 \\ z_2\\ \vdots \\z_n
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Then the values of $z$ will have the response:

$$z_i(t) = z_i(0)e^{\lambda _it}$$

Meaning that if $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$, then $\lim_{t\to \infty}z_i    \to 0$, i.e., it stable.
