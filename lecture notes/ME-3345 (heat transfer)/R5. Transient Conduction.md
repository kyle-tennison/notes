# R5. Transient Conduction

## Lumped Capacitance Model

Under the assumption of a **uniform temperature gradient** at any instant in a transient process (which implies infinite conduction).

The energy balance from the first law of thermodynamics is:

$$\dot E_{\rm cs} =\dot E_{\rm st}$$

In the book they show this as $- \dot E_{\rm out} = \dot E_{\rm st}$. Same thing. If the heat transfer out of the system is by convection, this can be expressed as:

$$-hA_s(T-T_\infty) = \rho Vc \frac{dT}{dt}$$

where the right hand side is really just the time derivative of the energy in a solid mass: $\Delta E_{st} = m \cdot c \Delta T$ . If we use the concept of [excess temperature](R3.%201D%20Steady%20State%20Conduction.md#^excess-temperature) and substitute $\theta = T-T_\infty$ into the equation above, it reduces to:

$$\frac{\rho V c}{hA_s} \frac{d\theta}{dt} = - \theta$$

Which solves to:

$$\frac{\rho V c}{hA_s}\ln \frac{\theta_i}{\theta} = t$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\theta}{\theta_i} = \frac{T-T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} = \exp \left[ - \frac{hA_s}{\rho Vc}\cdot t 
\frac{}{} \right]}$$

> $T_i = T(0)$, same with $\theta_i = \theta(0)$

You can express this using a time constant $\tau_t$ instead:

$$\tau _t = \left(\frac{1}{hA_s}\right)(\rho Vc) = R_tC_t$$

where $R_t$ is the [Thermal Resistance](R3.%201D%20Steady%20State%20Conduction.md#Thermal%20Resistance) and $C_t$ is the **lumped thermal capacitance** of the solid. 

If you want to get the total heat transfer, you can integrate this expression:

$$Q = \int _0^t q\,dt = hA_s \int_0^t \theta \,dt $$

Then, from the $\theta/\theta_i$ equation above, we can say:

$$\theta = \theta_i \exp(-t/\tau_t)$$

Expanding $\tau_t$ and integrating gives:

$$Q= (\rho Vc) \theta_i \left[ 1 - \exp \left(-\frac {t}{\tau}\right) \right]$$

And the opposite of this is the change in stored energy: $-Q = \Delta E_{\rm st}$. 

The "maximum possible thermal energy" refers to the maximum additional thermal energy added by $Q$ as $t\to \infty$. When this is the case, it reduces to:

$$\lim_{t \to \infty}Q(t) =\rho V c \theta_i$$

Hence, "accumulating 90% of the maximum thermal energy" will occur with $Q'$ where:

$$Q'=0.90\cdot Q(\infty) = 0.90 \cdot \rho V c \theta_i$$

This is where the expression 

$$\frac{Q'}{\rho c V \theta_i} = 0.90\quad \Rightarrow =1-\exp[-t/\tau] $$

comes from.


## Biot Number

![[IMG_2062.jpg]]

The **Biot Number** gives a number that quantifies how valid the approximation of a uniform temperature gradient is. As shown in the image above, a Biot number $\ll 1$ is a good approximation. 

The equation for the Biot number in the case of a solid loosing heat to convection is:

$$\frac{T_{s,1}-T_{s,2}}{T_{s,2}-T_\infty} = \frac{(L/kA)}{(1/hA)} = \frac{R_{\rm cond}}{R_{\rm conv}} = \frac{hL}{k} = Bi$$
^1

> $T_{s,1}$ and $T_{s,2}$ are surface temperatures shown in the figure above.

where $Bi$ is the Biot Number. As seen, it's just the ratio between the conduction and convection; it shows if conduction plays a prominent role. In general, we want a *Biot Number less than 0.1* when performing a lumped capacitance analysis.

The **characteristic length** $L_c$ is a ratio of the solid's volume to it's surface area and may be used to calculate the Biot Number:

$$Bi = \frac{hL_c}{k}, \qquad L_c \equiv \frac{V}{A_s}$$

In the [previous equation](#^1), the characteristic length was simply $L$, but defining a strict characteristic length accommodates this calculation for complex geometries. Page 245 gives some characteristic lengths for common shapes, along with some conservative values that differ from the equation above a bit.

The **Fourier Number** is a dimensionless time (?) which can be used with the Biot number to express the term:

$$\frac{hA_st}{\rho Vc}= Bi \cdot Fo,\qquad Fo \equiv \frac{\alpha t}{L_c^2}$$

This reduces the equation to:

$$\frac{\theta}{\theta_i} = \frac{T-T_\infty}{T_i-T_\infty } = \exp(- Bi \cdot Fo)$$

## Spatial Effects

When $Bi > 0.1$, the spatial effects are non-negligible. You can honestly just solve the [Heat Diffusion Equation](R2.%20Conduction.md#Heat%20Diffusion%20Equation) computationally, but in this book, we look at how the 1D analytical solution can be applied to a few different problems. This 1D heat equation, for reminder, is:

$$\frac{\partial ^2T}{\partial x^2} = \frac 1\alpha \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}$$

We then endeavor to find an analytical solution $T(x, t)$. 

To solve this first order partial differential equation, we need an initial condition and two boundary conditions. For transient problems, the initial condition is usually 

$$T(x, 0)=T_i$$

i.e. the temperature distribution at the start. The boundary conditions are at either end of the 1D probelm:

$$\left.\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_{x=0}=c_1, \qquad\left.\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_{x=L}=c_2$$
For a 1D wall at a fixed temperature on one side without heat transfer, with the other side exposed to conductive heat transfer, these reduce to:

$$\left.\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_{x=0}=0, \qquad k\left.\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_{x=L} = h [T(L, t)-T_\infty]$$

If $T(x, 0) = T_i$ and $T_i$ is a constant, then the *maximum possible temperature difference* is $T_i - T_{\infty}$. If we say:

$$\theta ^*  \equiv \frac{\theta}{\theta_i}= \frac{T-T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty}$$

Now we have limited $\theta ^* \in [0,1]$, *and* made it dimensionless,

We can do the same thing with position $x$:

$$x^* \equiv \frac xL$$

and time

$$t^* = \frac{\alpha t}{L^2} = Fo$$

which is just the previously mentioned *Fourier Number*.

All of these values are dimensionless and $\in [0,1]$. Using these numbers, we can re-express the initial and boundary conditions for the 1D wall (derived in [Spatial Effects](#Spatial%20Effects)) as:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \theta^*}{\partial x^{*2}} = \frac{\partial \theta^*}{\partial\, Fo}\qquad \text{(Equation to Solve)}$$

$$\theta^*(x^*,0)=1 \qquad \text{(Initial Condition)}$$

$$\left.\frac{\partial \theta ^*}{\partial x^*}\right|_{x^*=0}=0, \qquad \left.\frac{\partial \theta ^*}{\partial x^*}\right|_{x^*=1}=-Bi\,\theta^*(1,t^*) \qquad \text{(Boundary Conditions)}$$

## Semi-Infinite Solids

$$T(x\to \infty, t) = T_i$$

If two semi-infinite contacts are placed such that their only interfaces are in contact, the equilibrium temperature $T_s$ will be:

$$T_s =\frac{(\sqrt{k\rho c})_AT_{A,i} + (\sqrt{k \rho c})_BT_{B,i}}{(\sqrt{k\rho c})_A + (\sqrt{k \rho c})_B}$$

The value $(\sqrt{k\rho c})$ is known as **thermal effusivity**, and is analogous to "thermal inertia".
