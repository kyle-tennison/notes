## Laplace Transformations

By definition:

$$F(s)=\mathscr L[f(t)] = \int_0^\infty  e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$$

You can solve this by manually if needed:

---

For example, let:

$$\text{let}\quad f(t) = \begin{cases}0&t<0,\\Ae^{-\alpha t}& t\ge0.\end{cases}$$

Then, 

$$\begin{aligned}
\mathscr L[f(t)] &= A\cdot\int_0^\infty e^{-st}e^{-\alpha t} \,dt = A \cdot \int_0^\infty e^{-(\alpha+s)t} \, dt\\\\
&= A\cdot \frac{1}{s+\alpha}
\end{aligned}\\\\
$$

This is where the $\displaystyle \mathscr L\left[e^{-at}\right] = \frac{1}{s+a}$ rule comes from.
