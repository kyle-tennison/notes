## Thermal Resistance 

Analogous to electrical resistance, thermal resistance is defined as *the ratio of a driving potential to the corresponding heat transfer rate.* For steady state conduction, this is:

$$R_{\rm cond} = \frac{T_1-T_2}{q_x}=\frac{L}{kA} \qquad \text{(1D-Conduction)}$$
^thermal-resistance-eq

This is found by rearranging [Fourier's Law](R2.%20Conduction.md#Fourier's%20Law). 

The same analog can be produced for convection:

$$R_{\rm conv} = \frac{T_s-T_\infty}{q}=\frac{1}{hA}$$

or for radiation:

$$R_{\rm rad} = \frac{T_s-T_{\rm sur}}{q}=\frac{1}{h_rA}$$

where $h_r$ is the [Radiation Heat Transfer Coefficient](lecture%20notes/ME-3345%20(heat%20transfer)/R1.%20Introduction.md#Radiation%20Heat%20Transfer%20Coefficient). 


A **composite wall** is an instance of a collection of different thermal resistances in series:

![[IMG_1805.jpeg]]

### Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient

Sometimes it is convenient to work with a *overall heat transfer coefficient* $U$ that works with an expression analogous to [Newton's Law of Cooling](lecture%20notes/ME-3345%20(heat%20transfer)/R1.%20Introduction.md#Newton's%20Law%20of%20Cooling):

$$q_x = UA\Delta T,\qquad \text{where}\quad UA= \frac{1}{R_{\rm tot}}$$
### Series and Parallel Resistance

When these heat resistances are **in series**, the total equivalent resistance is:

$$\boxed{R_{\rm tot} = \sum_i R_i = \frac{\Delta T}{q} = \frac{1}{UA}}$$

![[IMG_09DCE41AC4CB-1.jpeg|409x468]]

**Parallel** thermal circuits cannot be  modeled with full accuracy in 1D; however, they can be a reasonable approach. The figure above shows how a simple parallel circuit can be approximated as two series circuits. However, as the difference of $|k_F-k_G|$ grows larger, the accuracy of this reduces.

### Contact Resistance

Between two contacts, there might be an inperfect bond and different temperatures on either surface. The resistance *per unit area* from this can be quantified as:

$$R_{\rm contact}'' = \frac{T_A-T_B}{q''_x}$$

 
## Integrated Conduction Analysis

### Derivation

Steady state Fourier's law in a simple form is:

$$q_x = -kA\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{q_x \Delta x}{A}= -k{\Delta T}$$

This makes the assumption that $A$ and $k$ are constants, which isn't always true. We can use integrals to better represent this by assuming that $A$ is a function of $x$ and $k$  is a function of $T$:


$$q_x \int_{x_0}^{x_1}\frac{dx}{A} =  -\int_{T_0}^{T_1}k(T)\,dT$$

Use this when area and/or $k$ are non-constant.

## Radial Systems 

The [heat equation](R2.%20Conduction.md#^heat-eq) in cylindrical coordinates is: 

$$\begin{gather}
\frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left(k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right) +\frac{\partial }{\partial y}\left(k \frac{\partial T}{\partial y}\right) + \frac{\partial }{\partial z}\left(k \frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right)+\dot q_{\rm gen} = \rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}\\
\text{\small Cartesian Coordinates}\\\\
{\large \Downarrow}\\\\
\frac 1r \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)+\frac 1{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial \phi}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right) + \dot q_{\rm gen} = \rho c_p 
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}\\
\text{\small Cylindrical Coordinates}\\
\end{gather}$$
^cylindrical-heat-eq

### Hollow Cylinder

Many radial systems only have heat transfer in the radial axis, and can therefore be represented as a 1D system. For a *hollow cylinder*, this is:

$$q_{\rm r, cond}  = -kA \frac{dT}{dr}= -k(2\pi rL)\frac{dT}{dr}$$


The [heat equation](#^cylindrical-heat-eq) without heat generation at steady state is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac 1r \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)+\frac 1{r^2} \cancel{\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial \phi}\right) + \cancel{\frac{\partial}{\partial z}} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right) + \dot q_{\rm gen} &= \rho c_p 
\cancel{\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}}\\\\

\frac 1r \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(kr \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right) &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

because the only heat transfer is in this radial axis. Integrating this twice and solving, gives:

$$T(r) = \frac{T_{s,1}-T_{s,2}}{\ln(r_1/r_2)}\ln\left(\frac{r}{r_2}\right) + T_{s,2}$$

where $T_{s,1}$ and $T_{s,2}$ are the temperatures at the inner and outer surfaces respectively, corresponding to $r_1$ and $r_2$ respectively. Substituting back into the heat rate equation gives:

$$q_{\rm r,cond}  = \frac{2\pi L k (T_{s,1}-T_{s,2})}{\ln(r_2/r_1)}$$

Then, using the 1D [thermal resistance equation](#^thermal-resistance-eq), we can get:

$$R_{\rm cond} = \frac{\ln(r_2/r_1)}{2\pi L k}$$

If we want to use the [Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient](#Overall%20Heat%20Transfer%20Coefficient) with composite radial system, we can define each $U$ however we want so long that the corresponding $A$ matches. One way of doing this is to choose to define the $U$s and $A$s at the inner radius of each composite layer. 

### Hollow Sphere

Fourier's law in this instance is:

$$q_r = -k (4 \pi r^2) \frac{dT}{dr}$$

Using [Integrated Conduction Analysis](#Integrated%20Conduction%20Analysis), this can be written as:

$$\frac{q_r}{4\pi} \int_{r_1}^{r_2} \frac{dr}{r^2} = - \int _{T_{s,1}}^{T_{s,2}} k(T)\,dT$$

where $r_1$ and $r_2$ denote the respective inner and outer surfaces at temperatures $T_{1,s}$ and $T_{2,s}$ respectively. 


Assuming a constant $k$ and solving gives:

$$q_r = \frac{ 4 \pi k (T_{s,1}-T_{s,2}) }{ (1/r_1) - (1/r_2) }$$

The [Thermal Resistance](#Thermal%20Resistance) can again be calculated to reveal:

$$R_{\rm sphere} = \frac{1}{4\pi k} \left(\frac{1}{r_1} - \frac 1{r_2} \right)$$

## Fins


Fins can take many forms, but in general, they are what we think of as heat sinks. They increase the rate of heat transfer to a working fluid by increasing the surface area in contact with the fluid. If we are careful with our equations and make some simplifying equations, we can treat fins as 1D heat transfer.

![[image-104.png]]

There are some (not all) of the types of fins. 

### Derivation

![](https://www.real-world-physics-problems.com/images/pin_fin_schematic.png)

> $A_c$ is cross section area, $A_s$ is surface area (if that wasn't clear).

From the conservation of energy at steady state, we know:

$$q(x) = q(x+dx)-dq_{\rm conv}$$

We can find expressions for each of these terms:

First, from Fourier's law, it is known that:

$$q(x) = -kA_c(x) \frac{dT}{dx}$$


Secondly, from Taylor series, we can approximate:

$$q(x+dx) \approx q(x)+ \frac{dq_x}{dx}dx = -kA_c (x) \frac{dT}{dx} - k \frac{d}{dx}\left( A_c(x)\frac{dT}{dx} \right) dx$$


And finally, the differential convective heat transfer $dq_{\rm conv}$ can be expressed as:

$$dq_{\rm conv} = dA_s h (T(x)-T_{\infty})$$


Altogether in $q(x)=q(x+dx) - dq_{\rm conv}$, this is:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\left( A_c(x) \frac{dT}{dx} \right) - \frac hk \frac{dA_s}{dx} (T(x)-T_\infty ) = 0
$$

Expanding the derivatives:

$$\underset{\text{General Fin Equation}}{\boxed{\frac{d^2T}{dx^2} + \left( \frac1{A_c(x)} \frac{dA_c}{dx} \right) \frac{dT}{dx} - \left( \frac{1}{A_c(x)}\frac hk \frac{dA_s}{dx} \right)(T(x)-T_\infty)=0}}$$

### Uniform Cross Section Fins

We can solve the general equation above relatively easily when the cross sections are uniform. The cross section area $A_c$ is constant with $x$ and $A_s=Px$ where $P$ is the cross section perimeter. This reduces $dA_c/dx \Rightarrow 0$ and $dA_s/dx \Rightarrow P$, giving the following:

$$\frac{d^2T}{dx^2} - \frac{hP}{kA_c}(T(x)-T_\infty)=0$$
