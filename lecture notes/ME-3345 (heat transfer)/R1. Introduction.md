# R1. Introduction

## Heat, Heat Flux, Heat Transfer


- $q$ is heat transfer, in units of energy.
- $q''$ is heat flux, in units of energy per unit area.
- Heat on its own doesn't mean anything; it needs to be transfered.

## Fourier's Law

Fourier's law for **conduction** is:

$$q_\rm{cond}'' = k \frac{dT}{dx}$$

more on it [here](R2.%20Conduction.md).

You can integrate this twice to get a temperature distribution.

## Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's law of cooling for **convection** is:

$$q''_{\rm conv} = h(T_b-T_\infty)$$

## Stephan Boltzmann

Stephan Boltzmann law for a small object radiating heat is:

$$q''_{\rm rad} = \epsilon \sigma (T_s^4 - T_{\rm surr}^4)$$

The limitations and details are provided in [Radiation Heat Transfer](lecture%20notes/ME-3345%20(heat%20transfer)/L1.%20Introduction.md#Radiation%20Heat%20Transfer).

### Radiation Heat Transfer Coefficient 

Sometimes it is advantageous to treat radiation like convection; this can be done by defining $h_r$ in a unique way such that it can be used with [Newton's Law of Cooling](#Newton's%20Law%20of%20Cooling):

$$h_r \equiv \epsilon \sigma (T_s+T_{\rm sur})(T_s^2+T_{\rm sur}^2)$$

then, we can use:

$$q''_{\rm rad}= h_r(T_s-T_{\rm sur})$$

This method is weak though because $h_r$ depends on both temperatures, and therefore cannot be treated as constant unless:

1. Both temperatures remain constant
2. The resulting error is acceptable
