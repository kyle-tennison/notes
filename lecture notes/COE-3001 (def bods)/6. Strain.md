# 6. Strain

## Displacement

Displacement is described by $u, v, w$ for $x, y, z$ respectively. Each one is a function of the coordinates: $u(x,y,z), v(x,y,z), w(x,y,z)$. 


### Translations

For a $\Delta x$ translation:

$$\cases{u(x,y,z)=\Delta x \\\\v(x,y,z)=0\\\\w(x,y,z)=0}$$

It's pretty easy to see how this would work in the other dimensions. 

### Rotations

For a rotation of $\theta$ in the $xy$ plane, the displacement about an origin $C = (x_c,y_c)$ is:

$$\cases{
u(x,y,z) = -(y-y_c)\sin(\theta) -(x-x_c)\big(1-\cos (\theta)\big)\\\\
v(x,y,z) = (x-x_c)\sin(\theta) - (y-y_c)\big(1-\cos (\theta)\big)\\\\
w(x,y,z)=0
}$$

Under the [small angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation) , $\sin(\theta) \approx \theta$ and $\cos (\theta) \approx 1$; this reduces the formula to:

$$\cases{
u(x,y,z) = -(y-y_c)\theta \\\\
v(x,y,z) = (x-x_c)\theta\\\\
w(x,y,z)=0
}$$


## Deformation

For reference:
![[image-57.png|439x309]]

**Normal strain** acts along the longitudinal axis and is:

$$\text{Normal Strain} = \epsilon _{xx} = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$$

**Engineering Shear Strain** $\gamma_{yx}=\gamma_{xy}$ is the change in angle (in radians) from the original shape. **Tensor shear strain** $\epsilon_{yx}=\epsilon_{xy}$ is the strain that shows up in the **strain tensor**, and the two are related by: $\epsilon_{yx}=\gamma_{yx}$ (i.e. *tensor shear strain is half of engineering shear strain*).

The aforementioned shear strain tensor is:

$$\text{2D Strain Tensor} \equiv \begin{bmatrix}
\epsilon_{xx}& \epsilon_{yx} \\
\epsilon_{xy}& \epsilon_{yy} \\
\end{bmatrix}$$

This is *not to be confused* with:

$$\text{2D Strain Tensor} \not \equiv \begin{bmatrix}
\epsilon_{xx}& \gamma_{yx} \\
\gamma_{xy}& \epsilon_{yy} \\
\end{bmatrix}$$

In 3D, this is:

$$
\text{3D Strain Tensor}=\begin{bmatrix}
\epsilon_{xx} & \epsilon_{xy} & \epsilon_{xz} \\
\epsilon_{xy} & \epsilon_{yy} & \epsilon_{yz} \\
\epsilon_{xz} & \epsilon_{yz} & \epsilon_{zz}
\end{bmatrix}
$$


### Strain from Displacement

By more formal definition, normal strains are defined by:

$$\epsilon_{xx} \equiv \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}, \quad \epsilon_{yy} \equiv \frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad \epsilon_{zz} \equiv \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}$$

And the *engineering* shear strains are defined by:

$$\gamma_{yx} = \gamma_{xy}\equiv \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} ,\quad \gamma_{zy}=\gamma_{yz} \equiv \frac{\partial v }{\partial z} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y},\quad \gamma_{zx} = \gamma_{xz} \equiv \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+ \frac{\partial w}{\partial x} $$


And, of course, shear strains are conversely:

$$\epsilon_{xy} = \epsilon_{yx} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{xy} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{yx}, \quad \epsilon_{yz} = \epsilon_{zy} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{yz} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{zy}, \quad \epsilon_{zx} = \epsilon_{xz} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{zx} = \frac{1}{2} \gamma_{xz}$$


## Simplifications

For a linear-elastic isotropic material under **uniaxial loading only**:

$$\epsilon_{xx} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}}{E}$$

where $E$ is **Young's modulus**. 

And:

$$2\epsilon_{xy}=\gamma_{xy} = \frac{\sigma_{xy}}{G}$$

where $G$ is the **Shear Modulus**. 

The **Poisson Ratio** $\nu$ allows us to find the displacement in other directions given the force in a single axis:

$$ \epsilon_{yy} = - \frac{\nu  \sigma_{xx}}{E} = - \nu \epsilon_{xx}, \qquad \epsilon_{zz} = - \frac{\nu \sigma_{xx}}{E}=-\nu\epsilon_{xx}$$

> This is a unique property of specifically *isotropic* materials. 

### 3D Constitutive Law for an Isotropic Material

If you have $\sigma_{xx}$:  
$$\epsilon_{xx} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{yy} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{xx}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{zz} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{xx}}{E}$$

If you have $\sigma_{yy}$:  
$$\epsilon_{xx} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{yy}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{yy} = \frac{\sigma_{yy}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{zz} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{yy}}{E}$$

If you have $\sigma_{zz}$:  
$$\epsilon_{xx} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{yy} = -\nu \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E}, \quad \epsilon_{zz} = \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E}$$

> Only true for a linear-elastic isotropic material under uniaxial loading. 


More generally, *not exclusive to uniaxial loading* is the equation:


$$
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
\epsilon_{xx} \\
\epsilon_{yy} \\
\epsilon_{zz} \\
\gamma_{yz} \\
\gamma_{xz} \\
\gamma_{xy}
\end{array}
\right\}
=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
\epsilon_{xx} \\
\epsilon_{yy} \\
\epsilon_{zz} \\
2 \epsilon_{yz} \\
2 \epsilon_{xz} \\
2 \epsilon_{xy}
\end{array}
\right\}
=
\left[
\begin{array}{cccccc}
\frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
-\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
-\frac{\nu}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G} & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G} & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G}
\end{array}
\right]
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
\sigma_{xx} \\
\sigma_{yy} \\
\sigma_{zz} \\
\sigma_{yz} \\
\sigma_{xz} \\
\sigma_{xy}
\end{array}
\right\}
$$
^flexibility-matrix

This is usually abbreviated to:

$$\{\epsilon \} = [S] \{\sigma \}$$

where $S$ is the **flexibility matrix** or the **compliance matrix**. 

In 2D, it is assumed that $\sigma_{zz}=\sigma_{yz}=\sigma_{xz}=0$ (or that they're negligibly close to zero). This reduces the equation to:

$$
\begin{Bmatrix}
\epsilon_{xx}\\[4pt]
\epsilon_{yy}\\[4pt]
\gamma_{xy}
\end{Bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 \\[6pt]
-\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & 0 \\[6pt]
0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{Bmatrix}
\sigma_{xx}\\[4pt]
\sigma_{yy}\\[4pt]
\sigma_{xy}
\end{Bmatrix}
$$

You can also go backwards to get stress from strain:

$$\begin{Bmatrix}
\sigma_{xx}\\[4pt]
\sigma_{yy}\\[4pt]
\sigma_{xy}
\end{Bmatrix}
=
\frac{E}{1 - \nu ^2}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \nu & 0\\
\nu & 1 & 0\\
0 & 0 & \frac{1 - \nu}{2}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{Bmatrix}
\epsilon_{xx}\\[4pt]
\epsilon_{yy}\\[4pt]
\gamma_{xy}
\end{Bmatrix}$$

This shows that 

$$\sigma_{xy} = \frac{E}{1-\nu^2} \cdot \gamma_{xy}\left(\frac{1 - \nu}{2}\right) = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}\cdot \gamma _{xy} = G\gamma_{xy}$$

thus: $\displaystyle G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}$. Again, only for isotropic materials.
