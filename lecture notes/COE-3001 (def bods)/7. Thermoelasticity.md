# 7. Thermoelasticity

> This does not cover changes in material properties due to temperature, just deformations.

---

For an *isotropic* material:

$$\epsilon_{xx} = \epsilon_{yy}=\epsilon_{zz} = \alpha\Delta T$$

where $\alpha$ is the **coefficient of thermal expansion** and $T$ is temperature. 

The stresses here are not absolute, they sum in addition to mechanical strains:

$$
\left\{
\begin{array}{c}
\epsilon_{xx} \\
\epsilon_{yy} \\
\gamma_{xy}
\end{array}
\right\}
=
\underbrace{
\left[
\begin{array}{ccc}
\frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 \\
-\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G}
\end{array}
\right]
\left\{
\begin{array}{c}
\sigma_{xx} \\
\sigma_{yy} \\
\sigma_{xy}
\end{array}
\right\}
}_{\text{Mechanical Strain}}
+
\underbrace{
\left\{
\begin{array}{c}
\alpha \Delta T \\
\alpha \Delta T \\
0
\end{array}
\right\}
}_{\text{Thermal Strain}}
$$

If a material is prevented from straining, then it will develop **thermal stresses**. Otherwise, *there is no stress associated with thermal expansion if the material is allowed to grow.*
