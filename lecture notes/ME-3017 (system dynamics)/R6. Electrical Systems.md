## Electric Elements

The charge $q$ defines current $i$ as:

$$i = \frac{dq}{dt}$$
Volts is the work per unit charge. 

An *active* element requires external energy; *passive* elements do not.

Ohm's law is $v=ir$, but not all elements necessarily have a linear relationship between voltage and current. Diodes are an example of this. These are called *nonlinear resistances.* A diode might have a profile like 

$$i = c_1(e^{c_2v}-1)$$

where $c_1$ and $c_2$ are constants dependent on the type of diode.

The power through a resistive circuit is $Ri^2 = v^2/R$.

---

**Capacitors** store charge. The charge in a capacitor is given by:

$$q = \int i\,dt$$
The charge stored in the capacitor is linearly related to its voltage:

$$v = \frac qC$$

where $C$ is the capacitance. A transient expression for $v$ is:

$$v = \frac 1C \int_0^ti\,dt + \frac{q_0}{C}$$

you can derive this by substituting $q = \int i\,dt$ and solving for the arbitrary constant when the bounds $[0,t]$ are used. $q_0$ is the charge at $t=0$.

The power stored in a cap is $\frac 12 C v^2$. 

---

**Inductance** is caused by a resistance to a changing magnetic flux. The inductance $L$ is related to the flux across the inductor $\phi$ and the current $i$ with:

$$L = \frac {N\phi} i$$

> N is the number of turns. The following is under the assumption that $N=1$.

This shows that, for constant inductance, $i \propto \phi$. The voltage across an inductor is:

$$v = L \frac{di}{dt}$$

The power stored in an inductor (as magnetic energy ?) is $\frac 12 Li^2$.

### Impedance Method

Impedance $Z$ is defined as:

$$Z(s) = \frac{V(s)}{I(s)}$$

The impedance for a resistor is simply $Z(s)=R$. Short proof:

$$V(s) = \mathscr L \{ i R\}= R \cdot I(s)\Rightarrow R = \frac{V(s)}{I(s)}=Z(s)$$

For a capacitor, it is $Z(s) = (Cs)^{-1}$. Proof:

$$V(s) = \mathscr L \left\{ \frac 1C \int_0^t i\,dt \right\}= \frac 1C \cdot \frac{I(s)}{s} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{Cs}=\frac{V(s)}{I(s)}=Z(s)$$

And for an inductor, it is:

$$V(s) = \mathscr L \left\{ L\frac{di}{dt} \right\}= L(sI(s) - \cancelto0{i(0)})\Rightarrow Ls = \frac{V(s)}{I(s)}=Z(s)$$

> Notice the assumption that $i_0=0$ for this to work.

Impedances in series sum like:

$$Z(s) = Z_1(s)+Z_2(s) = \sum_iZ_i(s)$$

Impedances in parallel sum like:

$$Z(s) = \left( \frac{1}{Z_1}+\frac{1}{Z_2} \right)^{-1}= \frac{1}{\displaystyle \sum_iZ_i^{-1}} $$

Then, just do voltage divider law and stuff to get the desired voltage.

## Force-Voltage Analogy

A forced mass-spring-damper will have the EOM of:

$$m \ddot x + b \dot x + kx = f_i$$

A series RCL circuit has the equation:

$$L \frac{di}{dt} + Ri + \frac 1C \int i\,dt = v_i$$

If you express the RCL equation in terms of charge $q$, you get:

$$L\ddot q + R\dot q + \frac 1C q = v_i$$
which is the exact same mathematically.

So you can do the following mapping:
- $f_i \to v_i$
- $m \to L$
- $b \to R$
- $k \to 1/C$
- $x \to q$
- $\dot x \to i$

So you can convert any mechanical system into an electrical system using this.

## DC Motors

We might wish to go *cross-domain* and model the electrical *and* mechanical response of a system with a single transfer function. This is analytically possible for simple systems. The first cross domain equation is:

$$\tau = K \cdot i_a$$

This gives the relationship between the mechanical torque and motor current based on the *motor-torque constant* $K$. In the $s$ domain it is:

$$T(s) = KI_a(s)$$

There's also the *back EMF* voltage, which is the induced voltage by the motor spinning. This is givenby:

$$e_b = K_b\cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt}$$

where $e_b$ is the back EMF (in volts). $K_b$ is the *back EMF constant*. In the $s$ domain this is:

$$E_b(s) = K_b \cdot s \Theta(s)$$

When you *solve the electrical circuit*, you should get some $E_a(s)$ input in terms of the electrical components and $E_b(s)$. 

Then, in the mechanical system, you should solve for $T(s)$ in terms of the mechanical components. Then you can replace this with the cross domain equation $KI_a(s)$. Then, you substitute $I_a$ or whatever equivalent current with the mechanical components in the expression for $E_a(s)$.

Once you do this, you can solve for the transfer function from the electrical input $E_a(s)$ to whatever mechanical output you want, which is often the angular displacement $\Theta(s)$.

The transfer function is:

$$\frac{\Theta(s)}{E_a(s)} = \frac{K}{(sL_a+R_a)(s^2J+sb)+KK_bs}$$

In many cases, the inductance is negligibly small compared to the resistance $L_a \ll R_a$; this allows the transfer function to be simplified to:

$$\frac{\Theta(s)}{E_a(s)}\approx \frac Ks \cdot \frac{1}{R(sJ+b)+KK_b}$$

You can reduce this further by defining a motor gain constant $K_m$ and a motor time constant $T_m$:

$$K_m = \frac{K}{R_ab+KK_b}, \qquad T_m = \frac{R_aJ}{R_ab+KK_b}$$

Which yields:

$$\frac{\Theta (s)}{E_a(s)} = \frac{K_m}{s(sT_m+1)}$$

If you instead solve for the angular velocity $\Omega(s) = s \Theta(s)$, the time-domain solution for a constant voltage $E$ is:

$$\omega(t) = K_mE(1-e^{-t/T_m})$$
