## Mean Estimation 

### Sample Mean

This is the most basic average:

$$M_n = \frac{X_1+X_2+\dots+X_n}{n}$$

As $n$ gets larger, the variance of the sample mean $M_n$ gets smaller:

$$\text{var}(M_n) =\frac{\text{var}(X_1)+\text{var}(X_2)+\dots\text{var}(X_n)}{n^2}$$

The sample mean $M_n$ is estimating the true mean $\mu$. 

If $\text{var}(X_i)= \sigma^2$,

$$\text{var}(M_n) = \frac{n\sigma^2}{n^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

Summarized

$$M_n \sim \text{Normal}\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \right).$$

### Sample Variance

Given the i.i.d observations $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$, we can find the underlying variance by first subtracting the mean to get:

$$\widetilde X_i = X_i - \mu,$$

and then using:

$$S_n^2 = \frac{\widetilde X_1^2+\widetilde X_2^2+\cdots +\widetilde X_n^2}{n}$$

which is the sample variance (an estimate of $\sigma ^2$). 

If the true mean $\mu$ is not known, you can replace $\mu$ with the sample mean $M_n$. To denote this change, you add a bar to $S_n \to \bar S_n$. Now this is:

$$\bar S_n^2 = \frac 1 n \sum ^n_{i=1}(X_i-M_n)^2.$$

The expectation of $\bar S_n^2$ is:

$$\mathbf E[\bar S_n^2]  = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$$

which reveals its bias, but as $n \to \infty$, the bias goes to zero. 

**Bessel's Correction** modifies $\bar S_n$ to $\widehat S_n$ so that the estimation is unbiased:

$$\widehat S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum^n_{i=1}(X_i-M_n)^2=\frac{n}{n-1}\bar S_n^2$$

## The Weak Law of Large Numbers

If $X \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma)$, and $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ are $n$ independent samples with the same distribution as $X$, it follows that, as $n \to \infty$, the sample mean $M_n$ of these n samples will approach the true mean $\mu$. 

This is the kinda ass proof from the notes:

We know:

$$M_n=\frac 1n (X_1+X_2+\cdots+X_n)$$

And (from somewhere) that:

$$\mathbf P(|M_n-\mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{n \epsilon^2}$$

Epsilon is an arbitrarily small number that obeys $\epsilon > 0$. As $n \to \infty$, the RHS $\to 0$, and we see that regardless of how small $\epsilon$ is, the "error" in the mean $|M_n - \mu| < \epsilon$.  

This above relationship $\mathbf P(|M_n-\mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{n \epsilon^2}$ comes from the *Markov* and *Chebyshev Inequalities.*

### Markov Inequality

Let $X \in \mathbb R_{>0}$ and allow $F_X(0)=0$, meaning $f_X(x)=0 \in (-\infty,0)$.

The Markov inequality states:

$$\boxed{\mathbf P(X\ge a) \le \frac{\mathbf E[X]}{a}\;\forall a>0.}$$

From this, we can see that the probability of X being greater than, say, 5x its mean is 1/5. 10x would be 1/10, and so forth. **This is true for all positive value distributions.**

Because this is an inequality, it will give you the *maximum probability*; however, in reality, the actual probability is often much smaller.

### Chebyshev Inequality

Let $X$ be a random variable with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma ^2$. Then,

$$\boxed{\mathbf P(|X-\mu| > c) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{c^2}\;\forall c>0.}$$

You can derive this from the [Markov Inequality](#Markov%20Inequality). 

Conceptually, same idea as Markov's; just lets us express it in terms of variance.


---

These two probabilities can be used to prove the weak law of large numbers (WLLN). The **strong law of large numbers** (SLLN) is:

$$\mathbf P(\lim_{n\to \infty}M_n = u)= 1$$

but this is harder to prove.

## Confidence Intervals 

The sample mean and variance are random variables themselves that converge as $n \to \infty$. However, when $n < \infty$, there is some degree of uncertainty in these variables that we wish to quantify. To do this, we use confidence intervals.

Confidence intervals/levels can be applied to any parameter estimate, but is most commonly applied to the mean. 

We select a **confidence level** $1- \alpha$ where $\alpha \in [0,1]$. Then, we create a *lower estimator* $M_n^-$ and an *upper estimator* $M_n^+$ such that:

$$\mathbf P(M_n^-\le\mu\le M_n^+)\ge 1-\alpha$$
The interval $[M_n^-, M_n^+]$ is a "$1-\alpha$ confidence interval." For instance, $\alpha=0.05$ gives a 95\% confidence interval. 

From the [Central Limit Theorem](3.%20General%20Random%20Variables.md#Central%20Limit%20Theorem) we know that $M_n \to \text{Normal}$ as $n \to \infty$. In turn,

$$\frac{M_n-\mu}{\sqrt{\text{var}(M_n)}} \to \text{Normal}(0,1)$$

When $n$ is large enough for the approximation of $\text{var}(M_n) \approx \widehat S^2_n/n$ to be reasonable, the confidence interval is:

$$\left[\quad M_n-z \frac{\widehat S_n}{\sqrt n}, \quad M_n+z\frac{\widehat S_n}{\sqrt n}\quad \right]$$

where $z = \Phi ^{-1}(1- \alpha/2)$. 

## Parameter Estimation

There are a sequence of observations $\mathbf X = \left\{X_1, X_2, X_3, \dots, X_n\right\}$. There is a joint pdf $f_{\bf X}(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n; \theta)$ that depends on this unknown parameter $\theta$. We want to find what $\theta$ likely is.


### Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE)

$$\widehat \theta_{\text{MLE}} = \underset{\theta}{\text{arg max}} \;f_{\bf X}(x_1,\dots,x_n;\theta).$$
What we are doing here is finding the value of $\theta$ which maximizes the joint PDF at the point we are interested in. For this to work, $X_1=x_1, X_2=x_2,\dots,$ etc., so $f_{\bf X}$ is really just a function of $\theta$. 

### Bayesian Inference

$\theta$ is NOT a random variable, so when we are estimating it, we have certain questions to ask. MLE finds the $\theta$ that maximizes this PMF, but this may not be what we are interested in. Furthermore, it doesn't make sense to find the "most probable" $\theta$, because $\theta$ is just a deterministic, fixed number. 

**In the Bayesian inference, we *do* treat $\theta$ as a random variable.** This seems more like an engineering trick than anything, idk.

Let $\Theta$ be a random variable with pdf $f_\Theta(\theta)$; this is called the **prior distribution.** Then, we observe a sequence of random variables $\mathbf X = \{X_1,\dots,X_n\}$ and capture the relationship with the pdf $f_{\boldsymbol X | \Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n | \theta)$. Then, we use [Bayes' Rule](3.%20General%20Random%20Variables.md#3.6%20The%20Continuous%20Bayes'%20Rule) to get:

$$f_{\Theta | \mathbf X}(\theta|x_1,\dots,x_n) = \frac{f_{\boldsymbol X | \Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n | \theta)f_\Theta(\theta)}{f_{\boldsymbol X}(x_1,\dots,x_n)}$$

This is called the **posterior distribution**; it reflects our knowledge after observing the data. Then we use an estimator to estimate; there are a few of these. 

The **maximum a posteriori** (MAP) estimate is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\widehat \theta_{\text{MAP}}&= \underset{\theta}{\text{arg max}}\;f_{\Theta | \mathbf X}(\theta|x_1,\dots,x_n)\\
&= \underset{\theta}{\text{arg max}}\; f_{\mathbf X|\Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n|\theta)f_\Theta(\theta)
\end{aligned}$$

We can ignore the denominator here because it does not depend on $\theta$. 

The **least mean-squared error** (LMS) estimate is:

$$\widehat \theta_{\text{LMS}} = \mathbf E[\Theta |x_1,\dots,x_n] = \int \theta f_{\Theta|\mathbf X}(\theta|x_1,\dots,x_n)\,d\theta$$
#### Example

$X$ is a random variable  $\text{Uniform}([0, \theta])$ that models how late I may be. Say we have some ballpark guess for $\theta$, and we say:

$$f_\Theta(\theta) = 
\begin{cases}1&0\le \theta \le 1,\\
0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}$$

If we are given $\theta$, and we know $X$ is uniform, it is easy to find the PDF:

$$f_{\mathbf X | \Theta} (x_1,\dots,x_n|\theta) = \begin{cases}1/\theta & 0 \le x \le \theta, \\0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Then from Bayes' rule, we can compute the posterior:

$$
\begin{aligned}  
f_{\Theta|\mathbf X}(\theta|x_1,\dots,x_n)  
&= \frac{f_{\mathbf X|\Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n|\theta) f_\Theta(\theta)}{f_{\mathbf X}(x_1,\dots,x_n)} \\  
&= \frac{f_{\mathbf X|\Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n|\theta) f_\Theta(\theta)}{\int_0^1 f_{\mathbf X|\Theta}(x_1,\dots,x_n|\theta') f_\Theta(\theta') , d\theta'} \\  
&= \frac{\frac{1}{\theta} }{\int_x^1 \frac{1}{\theta'} d\theta'} \\  
&= \frac{1}{\theta , |\log x|}, \quad 0 \le x \le \theta \le 1.  
\end{aligned}  $$

Then, using the MAP estimate, we get:

$$\widehat \theta_{\text{MAP}}=x$$

which coincides with the MLE.

The LMS estimate is:

$$\widehat \theta _\text{LMS} = \int_x^1 \theta \frac{1}{\theta | \log x|}$$

### Terminology

By definition $\Theta_n$ is the *estimator*, which is a single random variable that is a function of the observed data:

$$\widehat\Theta_n = g(X_1,\dots,X_n)$$

Even when we aren't treating $\theta$ as random, $\widehat\Theta_n$ is still a random variable. 

The **estimation error**, which is also a random variable, is:

$$\text{error}(\widehat\Theta_n) = \widehat\Theta_n - \theta$$

The **bias** of $\widehat \Theta_n$ is:

$$\text{bias}(\widehat \Theta_n) = \mathbf E[\text{error}(\widehat \Theta_n)] = \mathbf E[\widehat \Theta_n] - \theta$$

If the bias is zero, the estimator is said to be *unbiased.*

An estimator is **consistent** if it converges to $\theta$ as $n \to \infty$. 

The **mean-square error** (MSE) of an estimator, a common way to measure the performance of the estimator, is:

$$\text{MSE}=\mathbf E\left[(\text{error}(\widehat \Theta_n)^2 \right]=\mathbf E\left[(\widehat \Theta_n - \theta)^2 \right]= \text{bias}^2(\widehat \Theta_n) + \text{var}(\widehat \Theta_n)$$

$$H_{n,B} = \frac 1 n \sum^n_{i=1}\mathbf 1_{\displaystyle \small  z_{k-1}\le X_i\le z_k}.$$



### Kernel Density Estimation

![[image-72.png]]

The idea is you measure xs at different points, then put a bump centered at each of these points, then sum them together. These bums are called **kernels** and can be anything that satisfies the following criteria:

$$k(x)\ge0, \quad \text{and}\quad \int_{-\infty}^\infty k(x)\;dx =1$$

Then the **kernel density estimate** (KDE) is:

$$\hat f_k(x) = \frac 1 n \sum^n_{i=1}k(x-x_i)$$

One of these fits is the spline fit:

$$k_h(x) = \frac 1 h k \binom x{\overline h}, \quad h > 0$$
where $h$ is a tunable parameter that changes the "sharpness" of the bump. For noisy data, this can mean the difference between smoothing over everything, or visualizing the peaks. **Silverman's approximation rule** says to pick an $h$ value that is:

$$h = \frac{1.06 \sigma}{n^{1/5}}$$

## Classification and Hypothesis Testing

In classification, we have candidates $f_0(x)$ and $f_1(x)$, and for a given data point, we are trying to decide whether it came from $f_0$ or $f_1$. Depending on our conclusion, we assign a $Y$ value: $Y=0 | f_0, \quad Y=1 | f_1.$ 

Another (better) way to think of this (same meaning) is to have $Y \in \{0,1\}$ and $X \in \mathbb R^d$ such that:

$$f_0(x) = f_{X|Y}(x|Y=0), \quad f_1(x)=f_{X|Y}(x|Y=1).$$


Sometimes, it is assumed that $f_0$ and $f_1$ (and hence $f_{X|Y}$) is known. Other times, they are unknown; these spur two different types of problems.

### Bayes Classifier

When $f_{X,Y}$ is well defined and known (given $Y=0$ and $Y=1$) *and* $P(Y=1)$ is known, we can use [Bayes' Rule](3.%20General%20Random%20Variables.md#3.6%20The%20Continuous%20Bayes'%20Rule) to find $P(Y=1|X=x)$.

$$\begin{align}
\mathbf P(Y = 1|X = x) &= \frac{f_{X|Y}(x|Y = 1) \mathbf P(Y = 1)}{f_X(x)} \\
&= \frac{f_{X|Y}(x|Y = 1) \mathbf P(Y = 1)}{f_{X|Y}(x|Y = 0) \mathbf P(Y = 0) + f_{X|Y}(x|Y = 1) \mathbf P(Y = 1)}
\end{align}$$

This gives us the probability of $Y$ being either 1 or 0. To make your decision, just pick the maximum of $\mathbf P(Y=0|X=x)$ vs $\mathbf P(Y=1|X=x)$

This can be expanded beyond 1D.

More generally, beyond $\mathbb R^1$, we have a classification rule $h(X)$ that divides $\mathbb R^d$ into *two regions*, $\mathcal R_0$ and $\mathcal R_1$. 

$$\mathcal R_0 = \{ \boldsymbol x\in \mathbb R^d : h(\boldsymbol x) = 0 \}, \mathcal R_1 = \{ \boldsymbol x \in \mathbb R^d : h(\boldsymbol x) = 1 \}$$

The Bayes classifier is maximizing the probability $\mathbf P(h(X) = Y)$, i.e. the probability the classification rule $h$ is correct. 

## Learning a Classifier From Data

When $f_{X,Y}$ is unknown (as it often is in real life), we want to develop a classification rule from **labeled data.** That is, we are looking to find $\hat h(X)$. 


$$\hat h (X) = \begin{cases}
1 & X \in \mathcal R_1,\\ 
0 & X \in \mathcal R_2.
\end{cases}$$
This rule can really be anything. A *linear* classification rule draws a line in $\mathbb R^2$ that divides the data:

$$h(\boldsymbol x) = \begin{cases}
1 & \boldsymbol a \cdot \boldsymbol x + b \ge 0, \\
0 & \boldsymbol a \cdot \boldsymbol x + b < 0.
\end{cases}
$$

In $\mathbb R^1$ the most common way is to just divide the number line at a fixed point.

For higher dimensions, you can fit the posterior $\mathbf P(Y=1|X=x)$ using **logistic regression.**

### Logistic Regression

We're trying to fit a function $\eta (\boldsymbol x) = \mathbf P(Y=1 | X=x)$ given the data $\{(\boldsymbol x_i,y_i)\}^n_{i=1}$ . 

In the special case when the two classes have the same normal distribution variance but different means, $\eta$ is:

$$\eta (\boldsymbol x) = \mathbf P (Y=y|X=x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-(\boldsymbol a \cdot \boldsymbol x+b)}}= g(\boldsymbol a \cdot \boldsymbol x +b)$$

where $g$ is the **logit function:**

$$g(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$$
This is not always the case.

Now we have:

$$P(Y=y_i|X=\boldsymbol x_i) = \begin{cases}
\eta(\mathbf x_i) & y_i=1\\
1- \eta (\mathbf x_i) & y_i=0.
\end{cases}$$

Then you just maximize the corresponding probabilities to find the parameters of $\eta$. When $\eta$ is logit, this is $\boldsymbol a$ and $b$. You usually need some algorithm to do this.

### Linear Regression

Trying to find a function $f(x_i) = \theta_0 + \theta _1x_i \approx y_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, n$. 

Let
$$Y_i \sim \text{Normal}(\theta_0 + \theta_1 x_i, \sigma^2).$$

Now we have the likelihood function

$$L(\theta_0, \theta_1) = f_{\mathbf Y}(y_1, \dots, y_n; \theta_0, \theta_1) = \prod ^n_{i=1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi} \; \sigma}\cdot \exp\left(\frac{-(y_i-\theta_0-\theta_1x_1)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

> You would take the log of this to make it simpler. Shown on p.80 of notes

This becomes an optimization problem: we want to maximize this liklihood function (im so lost). You can do this by expanding the equation and setting the partial derivatives to zero.

The solution to this is:

$$\widehat \theta_1 = \frac{ \sum ^n_{i=1}(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{ \sum^n_{i=1}(x_i-\bar x)^2}, \quad \widehat \theta_0 = \bar y - \widehat \theta_1 \bar x.$$

where the barred values are the means.

### Polynomial Regression

These notes describe the same method as discussed in [Polynomial Regression](Regression.md#Polynomial%20Regression) from ME2016. 

Setup a system:

$$\begin{bmatrix}
y_0 \\ y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_n
\end{bmatrix}

= 
\begin{bmatrix}

z_{00} & z_{01} & z_{02} & \cdots & z_{0m} \\
z_{10} & z_{11} & z_{12} & \cdots & z_{1m} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
z_{n0} & z_{n1} & z_{n2} & \cdots & z_{nm} \\

\end{bmatrix}

\begin{bmatrix}
a_0 \\ a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\a_n
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Where $z_{ij}$ is $x_i^j$. In english: first subscript is point index, j is the power it's raised to.

The equation to solve is:

$$\textbf Z ^\intercal \textbf Z \;\vec a = \textbf Z^\intercal \; \vec y$$

If you choose to solve via simple inversion, it is:

$$\vec a  = \big(\mathbf Z^{\rm T}\mathbf Z\big)^{-1} \mathbf Z^{\rm T}y$$

> $a$ is analogous to $\theta$ in the previous example.

Then, the regression function is:



$$\hat f(x)= a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_m x^m.$$
