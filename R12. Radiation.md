# R12. Radiation

## Fundamentals

The infrared wavelength is $\lambda \in [ 10^{-1}, 10^2]\mu m$ .

The *irradiation* $G$ (i.e. the incident radiation per unit area) can either be absorbed, reflected, or transmitted.


$$\begin{aligned}G &= G_{\rm ref} + G_{\rm abs} + G_{\rm tran}\\&=\zeta G + \alpha G + \tau G\end{aligned}$$

where:
- $\zeta$ is reflectivity
- $\alpha$ is absorptivity
- $\tau$ is transmissivity
- $\zeta + \alpha + \tau = 1$

all are $\in [0,1]$

Opaque mediums have $\tau=0$.

The **Radiosity** $J$ represents the rate at which radiation leaves a surface (per unit area). This is the sum of:
1. Reflected radiation $G_{\rm ref}$
2. Emitted radiation $E$

$$J = E+G_{\rm ref} = E+\zeta G$$
^radiosity
> In the case of a translucent material, there would be another term for radiation transmitted from the other side.

The emitted radiation $E$ is from the [Stefan Boltzmann Law](lecture%20notes/ME-3345%20(heat%20transfer)/L1.%20Introduction.md#^emmited-radiation), which says:

$$E = \epsilon \sigma T^4 $$

The net flux *leaving* the surface is:

$$\begin{aligned}
q''_{\rm rad} &= J - G \\
&= E+ \zeta G- G \\
&= E-(1-\zeta)G\\
&= \epsilon \sigma T_s^4 -  \alpha G
\end{aligned}$$

**Radiation Intensity** covers the directional influence of radiation, but that is not covered in this course.


There's a key difference between:
- $E_\lambda$, which is the the hemispherical emissive power per unit wavelength
- $E$, is the total hemispherical power

$$E = \int_0^\infty E_\lambda (\lambda) \, d\lambda$$

The same is true for the total irradiation:

$$G = \int_0^\infty G_\lambda(\lambda) \,d\lambda$$

## Blackbody Radiation

Synopsis:
- A blackbody *absorbs all incident radiation* (regardless of wavelength or direction).
- Emitted radiation is a function of only $\lambda$ and $T$—not direction.
- No other body at the same $\lambda$ and $T$ could emit more radiation. $\epsilon=1$ such that $E_b=\sigma T^4$.

> The $b$ subscript is for blackbody


The closest thing next to a black body in real life is a cavity, because it reflects the light inside itself many times before reflecting a fraction of it. 

The **Planck Distribution** describes the radiation emission as an analytical function:

$$\boxed{E_{\lambda,b}(\lambda , T)=\frac{C_1}{\displaystyle\small  \lambda^5\left[ 
\exp(\frac{C_2}{\lambda T}-1)
\right]}}$$

where:
$$
\begin{gather}
C_1= 2 \pi h c_0^2 \approx3.742\times10^8 \quad \frac{\rm W \cdot \mu m^4}{\rm m^2}\\
C_2 = \frac{hc_0}{k}\approx 1.439 \times 10^4 \quad {\rm\mu m\cdot K}
\\\vdots \\
h=6.6256 \times 10^{-34}\quad {\rm J\cdot s} \Leftarrow\text{ (Planck's Constant)}
\\
c_0 = 2.998\times10^8 \quad {\rm m/s} \Leftarrow \text{(Vacuum Speed of Light)}\\
k = 1.3805\times 10^{-23} \quad {\rm J/K} \Leftarrow \text{(Boltzmann Constant)}
\end{gather}
$$

### Wein's Displacement Law

$$\lambda_{\max}T=C_3$$

where $C_3 = 2897.8 {\rm \mu m \cdot K}$. This lets you find the $\lambda$ that maximizes emissive power for a given temperature $T$.

### Band Emission

The band emission gives the total emission at a particular interval or "band" of wavelengths. It's just the fraction of the integral:

$$F_{(0\to \lambda)} = \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\lambda  E_{\lambda,b}\,d\lambda } {\displaystyle \int_0^\infty E_{\lambda,b}\,d\lambda}= \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\lambda  E_{\lambda,b}\,d\lambda } {\displaystyle \sigma T^4} $$

This is tabulated as a function of $T \cdot \lambda$. If you want to find the *band between two nonzero wavelengths* $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, you can use:

$$F_{(\lambda_1\to \lambda_2)}=F_{(0 \to \lambda_2)}-F_{(0\to\lambda_1)}$$

## Spectral Reflection, Absorption, and Transmission


The spectral emissivity is $\epsilon_\lambda(\lambda,T)$, which is not the same as the emissivity $\epsilon$ that was talked about hitherto. The *total emissitivty* $\epsilon$ is related to $\epsilon_\lambda$ with:

$$\epsilon(T) =\frac{E(T)}{E_b(T)}= \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty \epsilon_\lambda (\lambda,T)E_{\lambda,b}(\lambda,T)}{E_b(T)}$$
As seen here, $\epsilon$ is just a percentage of the blackbody radiation that some other body has.

---

A similar approach is used for $\alpha, \tau, \zeta$:

$$\alpha_\gamma(\gamma) = \frac{G_{\rm \lambda,abs}(\lambda)}{G_\lambda(\lambda)},\quad \Rightarrow \quad \alpha = \frac{G_{\rm abs}}{G}= \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty \alpha_\lambda(\lambda) G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}$$

where $\rm abs$ is short for absorbed.

Basically the exact same is done for transmissivity $\tau$ and reflectivity $\zeta$:

$$\tau_\lambda(\lambda) = \frac{G_{\rm \lambda,tr}(\lambda)}{G_\lambda(\lambda)},\quad \Rightarrow \quad \tau = \frac{G_{\rm tr}}{G}= \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty \tau_\lambda(\lambda) G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}$$

$$\zeta_\lambda(\lambda) = \frac{G_{\rm \lambda,ref}(\lambda)}{G_\lambda(\lambda)},\quad \Rightarrow \quad \zeta = \frac{G_{\rm ref}}{G}= \frac{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty \zeta_\lambda(\lambda) G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}{\displaystyle \int_0^\infty G_\lambda(\lambda)\,d\lambda}$$

At any $\lambda$, it is still true that:

$$\alpha_\lambda + \zeta _\lambda + \tau_\lambda = 1$$

## Kirchhoff's Law

If a large isothermal surface of temperature $T_s$ confines several small bodies of negligibly small size, then the irradiation is diffuse (i.e., not dependent on direction). The total irradiation is then a function of this temperature only:

$$G=E_b(T_s)$$
