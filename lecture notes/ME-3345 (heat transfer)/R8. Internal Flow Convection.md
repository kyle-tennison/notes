# R8. Internal Flow Convection

## Hydrodynamic Flow Development

[Various Flow in Pipes](8.%20Various%20Flow%20in%20Pipes.md) is a good reference/pre-req.

In DeWitt, the method for characterizing the development of flow differs slightly from the fluids book. I'll cover the DeWitt method here.

---

The Reynold's number through a tube with cross section diameter $D$ is:

$$Re_D = \frac{\rho u_m D}{\mu}$$

where $u_m$ is the mean velocity across the cross section. 

For laminar flow $Re_D \lesssim 2300$ the entrance length $x_{\rm fd,h}$ can be found with:

$$\left(\frac{x_{\rm fd,h}}{D}\right)_{\rm lam} \approx 0.05Re_D$$


For turbulent flow, no general expression exists, but it is generally assumed that

$$10 \lesssim \left(\frac{x_{\rm fd,h}}{D} \right)_{\rm turb} \lesssim 60$$

For this course, assume $x_{\rm fd,h}\approx 10D$. 

---

The mean velocity $u_m$ can be used to develop a mass rate equation:

$$\dot m = \rho u_m A_c$$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density and $A_c$ is the cross section area. 

For reference, if $u$ is known as a polar function for a pipe of radius $r_0$:

$$u_m = \frac{2}{r_0^2}\int _0^{r_0}u(r,x)r\,dr$$

## Thermal Considerations in Flow Development

As shown in the [figure](#^development-fig) above, the temperature gradient develops alongside the velocity gradient. Depending on the boundary condition, the thermal profile will develop to to different final profiles. Generally, this is one of two:

1. A fixed heat flux at the cylinder wall
2. A uniform temperature at the cylinder wall

The length it takes for the thermal layer to fully develop $x_{\rm fd,t}$ is not necessarily the same as the hydrodynamic entrance length $x_{\rm fd,h}$. Instead,

$$\left(\frac{x_{\rm fd,t}}{D}  \right)_{\rm lam}\approx 0.05Re_DPr$$

where $Pr$ is the [Prandtl Number](R6.%20Convection.md#^prandtl-number). 

For turbulent flows (low $Pr$), $x_{\rm fd,t}$ is largely independent of $Pr$ and it is assumed $x_{\rm fd,t}\approx 10D$. 

In [External Flow Convection](R7.%20External%20Flow%20Convection.md) the free stream temperature is used to fix the flow temperature; but because there is no free stream, the **mean temperature** must be used. This is also why the mean velocity was used above; there is no free-stream velocity to reference. 

The mean temperature $T_m$ is defined such that:

$$T_m = \frac{2}{u_mr_o^2}\int _0^{r_0}uTr\,dr$$

[Newton's Law of Cooling](lecture%20notes/ME-3345%20(heat%20transfer)/R1.%20Introduction.md#Newton's%20Law%20of%20Cooling) can be used using this mean temperature:

$$q''_s = h(T_s-T_m)$$

However, it is important to note that:
1. $h$ is the *local* convection coefficient 
2. $T_m$, unlike $T_\infty$, is nonconstant; i.e. $\partial T_m/\partial x \ne 0$. 

## Energy Balance

*If* in the fluid flowing through the pipe, the change in fluid potential and kinetic energy, along with axial-direction conduction is *negligible*, then the two primary energy factors are:

1. Flow work to move the fluid through the pipe
2. Thermal energy changes

By using a differential element in the pipe, we can discover how $T_m$ varies with $x$.

![](https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcROnHdE8k8FIEDa27tvwN8OVEVQeWljSYKr3g&s)
[^2]

A first law balance shows[^3]:

$$dq_{conv} = \dot m\left[ d(c_vT_m) + d(pv)\right]$$
where $v$ is the specific volume $1/\rho$.

If the fluid is an **ideal gas**, then $pv=RT_m$ and $c_p = c_v+R=\text{const}$. So:



$$
\begin{gather}
\begin{aligned}
dq_{conv} &= \dot m\left[ d(c_vT_m + pv)\right]\\
 &=\dot m\left[ d( c_p-R) T_m + RT_m)\right]\\
 &=\dot m\left[  d(T_m(c_p-R+R))  \right] = \dot m\left[  d(T_mc_p)  \right]\\
 
\end{aligned}\\ \\
\boxed{dq_{conv}= \dot m c_p\,dT_m}
\end{gather}
$$

Integrating this over the entire tube gives:

$$\boxed{q_{conv} = \dot m c_p (T_{\rm m,out} - T_{\rm m, in})}$$

where $q_{conv}$ is the total heat transferred to/from the system.  

The heat transfer $dq_{conv}(x)$ may be expressed instead using the heat flux because:

$$dq_{conv} (x) = q_s''P\,dx$$

where $P=\pi D$ (permimiter) and $q_s''$ is the heat flux as a function of $x$. This enables the following equality:

$$\boxed{\frac{dT_m}{dx} = \frac{q_s''P}{\dot m c_p} = \frac{P}{\dot m c_p}h(T_s-T_m)} \tag 1$$
^internal-flow-ode

This equation is not exclusive to constant cross-sections; $P$ may vary with $x$. However, if it doesn't and flow is steady, the entire $P/\dot m c_p$ term is constant.

Furthermore, *the convection coefficient is constant in the fully developed region*[^4] (although it does vary in the entrance region—it starts high and drops when flow develops). 

### Constant Heat Flux Case

With a constant heat flux, it is simple to solve the [ODE](#^internal-flow-ode) (1) above. But first, if all that is wanted is the total heat transfer, the relationship is simply:

$$q_{conv} = q''PL$$

where $P$ is again the perimeter and $L$ is the length of the pipe. This assumes constant cross-section.

From the [ODE](#^internal-flow-ode), it can be seen that, for a uniform cross-section tube, the term $\frac{q_s''P}{\dot m c_p}$ is wholly independent of $x$ *and* constant. Therefore, the derivative is literally given directly. Integration from the inlet to $x$ gives:

$$\boxed{T_m(x) = T_{m,i} + \frac{q_s''P}{\dot mc_p}x\qquad(\text{for constant } q''_s)}$$


### Constant Surface Temperature Case

Solving the [ODE](#^internal-flow-ode) with constant surface temperature is slightly different. First, let:

$$\Delta T = T_s-T_m$$

This is similar (ish) to the [excess temperature](R3.%201D%20Steady%20State%20Conduction.md#^excess-temperature). In the constant surface temperature case (i.e. this), this is just a constant offset (and sign flip)—just like excess temperature.

It just makes the math easier. Substituting this into the ODE gives:

$$\frac{d(\Delta T)}{dx} = \frac{P}{\dot mc_p} h \Delta T$$

Solving via separation of variables:

$$\int_{\Delta T_i}^{\Delta T'}\frac{d(\Delta T)}{\Delta T}  = - \frac{P}{\dot m c_p}\int_0^{x'} h\,dx$$

> The reason $h$ didn't show up in the [Constant Heat Flux Case](#Constant%20Heat%20Flux%20Case) is because it is assumed to be constant (for fully developed flow), and I suppose $q''_s$ takes care of the rest.

This works out to be:

$$\ln \left[\frac{\Delta T(x)}{\Delta T_i}\right] = - \frac{Px\bar h}{\dot mc_p}$$

or more conveniently:

$$\boxed{\frac{\Delta T(x)}{\Delta T_i}=\frac{T_s-T_m(x)}{T_s-T_{m,i}}= \exp \left[-\frac{Px\bar h}{\dot mc_p}\right] \qquad (\text{for constant }T_s)}$$

where $\bar h$ is the average $h$ *up to* $x$ (nonconstant).

The total heat transfer is tricky due to the decay of $T_m$. By defining a *logarithmic mean* temperature $\Delta T_{\rm lm}$, we can make things easier:

$$\Delta T_{\rm lm} \equiv \frac{\Delta T_o - \Delta T_i}{\ln(\Delta T_o/\Delta T_i)}, \quad q_{conv} = \bar h A_s\Delta T_{\rm lm}$$

> I don't think that this is dependent on being in the fully developed region. I believe it applies for both.


#### External Fluid

If there is a separate *external* fluid flowing over the pipe with free-stream temperature $T_\infty$, then this relationship can still be used by switching $T_s \to T_\infty$ and $\bar h \to \bar U$ (where $U$ is the average overall heat transfer coefficient). Literally everything else is the same.

The [Constant Heat Flux Case](#Constant%20Heat%20Flux%20Case) will have a constant slope in the fully developed region. Before this, when the flow is turbulent, $h_{\rm turb} > h_{\rm lam}$ and because $dT_m/dx \propto h$, the slope is higher at the beginning. 

The [Constant Surface Temperature Case](#Constant%20Surface%20Temperature%20Case) case will exponentially approach $T_s$ , decaying as it nears.

## Laminar Flow Convection Coefficients

In the above sections, we need to determine $h$ before we can solve for the temperature distribution. For laminar flow, we may theoretically develop some relationships.

Skipping the derivation, the [Nusselt Number](R6.%20Convection.md#^nusselt-number) $Nu_D =\frac{hD}k$ is either:

$$Nu_D =4.36 \quad(\text{constant }q''_s)$$

when heat flux is constant, or

$$Nu_D = 3.66 \quad(\text{contant } T_s)$$

when surface temperature is constant. When using these to determine $h$, evaluate fluid properties at:

$$T_m = \frac{T_{m,i}+T_{m,o}}{2}$$
That is, the average mean temperature[^6].
