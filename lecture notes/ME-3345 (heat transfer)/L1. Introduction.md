# L1. Introduction

Modes of heat transfer:
- **Conduction**
	- Temperature differences in a *stationary* medium.
	- This can be through a solid, liquid, or gas. (or more?)
- **Convection**
	- Heat transfer between a surface and moving fluid at different temperatures.
- **Radiation**
	- Electromagnetic radiation to transfer heat. All surfaces at $\rm T > 0 \rm K$ radiate some heat. 

> This is at the macroscopic level; there are weird things when you consider relativistic/quantum effects.



**"Phonons"** refer to lattice vibrations that are responsible for the heat

### Heat Transfer Rate

$$q = \dot Q = \frac{dQ}{dt}$$

where $Q$ is heat.

The heat transfer flux is:

$$\text{Heat Rate Flux}=\iint_S (q \cdot \hat{\bf n}) \;dS \underset{\text{More simply}}{\quad\Rightarrow\quad} \frac{\text{Rate}}{\text{Area}}$$
The notation is:

- $q$ for heat transfer rate. $\dot = \rm W$ 
- $q'$ for heat transfer per unit length. $\dot = \rm W/\rm m$
- $\mathbf q''$ for heat transfer per unit area (*heat flux*). $\dot = \rm W / \rm m^2$

Not to be confused with derivative notation: $\dot q \ne q'$. 

### Conductive Heat Transfer

**Fourier's Law** is:

$$\mathbf q'' = -k \nabla T$$

where
- $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material
- $T$ is the temperature field

this gives a *vector field*.

The 1D version is:

$$q_x'' = -k \frac{dT}{dx}$$

> K values for common materials are in the back of the book and in the CRC (probably)

For a steady state problem, $dT/dx$ can be written as:

$$\frac{dT}{dx}=\frac{T_1-T_2}{L}$$

### Radiation Heat Transfer

The Stefan Boltzmann Law is:

$$E_B = \epsilon \sigma T_s^4 $$

where $\epsilon$ is emissivity, $\sigma$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, and $T_s$ is the temperature of the surface. This is in units of $\rm W / \rm m^2$. 

In the case of a black body, $\epsilon=1$, which is the *maximum* emissivity of any surface. For all other surfaces, emissivity is $\epsilon \in [0,1]$. 

The **Incident Radiation** (per unit area) (aka *Irradiation*), which describes the radiation from the surroundings. The actual amount that is absorbed is given by:

$$G_{\rm abs} = \alpha G_{\rm total}$$

where $\alpha$ is *absorptivity* and is $\in [0,1]$.

$$\begin{aligned}G &= G_{\rm ref} + G_{\rm abs} + G_{\rm tran}\\&=\zeta G + \alpha G + \tau G\end{aligned}$$

where:
- $\zeta$ is reflectivity
- $\alpha$ is absorptivity
- $\tau$ is transmissivity

all are $\in [0,1]$

For the special case of a small surface surrounded by a large temperature reservoir:

$$q''_{\rm rad} = \epsilon \sigma T_s^4 - (\cdot) \sigma T_{\rm surr}^4$$

where $(\cdot)$ is some constant that encapsulates how much this small surface absorbs from the environment. In this special case of a small surface, this is actually $\epsilon$. 
Thus, this reduces to:

$$\underset {\begin{aligned}&\textbf{Stefan Boltzmann Law} \\ &\text{For a small surface in a large enclosure} \end{aligned}}{\boxed{q''_{\rm rad} = \epsilon \sigma (T_s^4 - T_{\rm surr}^4)}}$$

where
- $T_s$ is the temperature of the surface of interest
- $T_{\rm surr}$ is the temperature of the surroundings
- $\epsilon$ is the emissivity of the object
- $\sigma$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant
