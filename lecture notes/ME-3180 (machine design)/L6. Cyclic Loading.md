## Cycle Count

As the number of cycles $N$ goes to $\infty$, the strength of the material decreases.

For **infinite life** materials, the strength converges to some $S_e$ after $N\sim \infty$ . Steel is one of these materials, and the $S(N)$ diagram flattens to $S_e$ around $10^6$ loads. 

> A note on notation: $N_f$ is for factor of safety, $N$ is for load count.


## Modified Goodman

When doing a failure analysis under cyclic loading (at least in this class), we use modified goodman, regardless of if the material is even/uneven or brittle/ductile.

$$N_f = \frac{S_eS_{ut}}{\sigma'_a(S_{ut})+\sigma'_m (S_e)}$$

where $\sigma'_a$ and $\sigma_m'$ are the von mises of $\sigma_a$ and $\sigma_m$. (Not sure how).

Use $S_e = S(N)$ if $N$ is known. Otherwise, if the material is infinite life, default to $S_e = \lim_{N \to \infty} S(N)$. For finite-life materials, I'm not sure.

## $k$-factors

> These are **only valid for steel**

The value for $S_e'$ we might find from a plot could be too optimistic. Instead, we need to correct it with $k$ factors:

$$S_e = k_ak_bk_ck_dk_eS_e'$$

where:

- $k_a$ is the surface factor
- $k_b$ is the size factor
- $k_c$ is the load factor
- $k_d$ is the temperature factor
- $k_e$ is the reliability factor
- $S_e'$  is the test specimen fatigue strength
- $S_e$ is the fatigue strength at the critical point.

All $k$ values are less than or equal to $1$, so $S_e$ can never be bigger than $S_e'$. 

> Apparently, whenever $S_e'$ is big, you ceil it at $100 \rm {ksi}$ for some reason that he doesn't care to share.

Just use this doc here to figure this out: https://gatech.instructure.com/courses/507208/files/69729263?module_item_id=5717352
